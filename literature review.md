# Literature Review

Recent research on AI-based wireless communications has indicated that artificial intelligence plays a key role in the development of future 6G communications due to its potential to enhance efficiency. However, most of the existing literature on the use of artificial intelligence in wireless communication has focused on its potential to enhance sustainable wireless communication by reducing power wastage. However, recent research on the use of artificial intelligence has indicated that its computation has raised new environmental concerns.


A significant research paper written by Tariq A. Gulliver and Nasser Alhussien reveals that AI plays a role in the development of green 6G networking as it is able to dynamically manage the radio resources, predict the network demands, and minimize the idle energy consumption of the network devices. As the research reveals, intelligent management is able to adjust the transmission behavior of the network based on the real-time network conditions rather than the power settings.


Kamran et al. (2025) identify sustainability as a core requirement in future 6G network design. The survey states that "AI and machine learning techniques are increasingly being used in 6G network design due to their ability to find the optimal balance between quality of service and energy reduction." It also highlights that "in future 6G networks, it is expected that energy monitoring, renewable energy integration, and AI will be part of the network architecture, not just part of the optimization layers." It also states that "while AI can improve decision-making, it also incurs additional overheads in terms of computation, signaling, etc."


The environmental impact of AI itself has been a highly debated issue outside the field of telecommunications. A highly cited paper by Emma Strubell, Ananya Ganesh, and Andrew McCallum found that training large deep learning models uses considerable computational energy and results in measurable carbon emissions. The authors' research on the ACL conference found that improved model performance comes with a high computational cost due to repeated training, large hardware clusters, and parameter tuning. While this research focused on natural language processing, this same computational issue exists for wireless AI systems that use repeated training and large-scale inference.


Across recent literature, researchers consistently agree that AI offers important operational benefits for wireless sustainability, but those benefits depend on how efficiently AI models are designed and deployed. This creates a research gap: while AI improves communication efficiency, its own lifecycle energy demand must also be included in sustainability evaluation.
